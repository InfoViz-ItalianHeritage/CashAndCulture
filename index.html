Cash & Culture: Who Gets Funded, Who Gets Visited

Do you know which region in Italy has received the highest funding for cultural heritage in the past 10 years (2014-2024)? Do you think the same region was visited the most for its cultural heritage assets?

Get Started

About the Project

This project analyzes the distribution of public funding for cultural heritage and tourism projects across Italian regions, and compares those investments with the number of visitors each region received in the same years (2014 → 2024).

The goal is to trace the connection, mismatches (e.g., high funding / low visitation or vice versa), temporal trends, and possible explanations that can lead to policy discussion and further research.

Read More

Research Questions

How have public investments in cultural heritage been distributed across Italian regions over the past decade, and what relationship can be observed between funding allocation and public participation in cultural heritage activities?

Sub-question 1

Which years/regions (in 2014-2024) witnessed the highest amount of funding (public) allocated to cultural heritage?

Sub-question 2

Which regions received the highest share of funding each year?

Sub-question 3

How were the regional visitors' participation in cultural heritage events/activities?

Picking the data

In the preparation phase, we structured our approach around the central research questions and defined the scope of the analysis. Our objective was to explore how public funding for cultural heritage and tourism projects (2014 - 2024) was distributed across Italian regions and how these investments compared with the actual number of visitors in the same period. Key tasks included:

  • Identifying candidate data sources
    We surveyed multiple public datasets that might provide insight into cultural funding, heritage, tourism, and social or cultural participation:
    • The ArCo Knowledge Graph (to query dati.beniculturali.it), which provides linked open data on Italian cultural heritage through ontologies and catalog records.
    • OpenCoesione _ Culture & Tourism Project Data (2014–2020, 2021–2027)
    • Ministero della Cultura _ Cultural income statistics (MiC Income)
    • Ministero della Cultura _ Visitor and Participation Statistics (MIC Visitors)
    • The ISTAT “Aspects of Daily Life” (AVQ) survey, which captures household- and individual-level variables on cultural consumption, social participation, and quality-of-life metrics.
  • Evaluating relevance & feasibility
    For each candidate dataset, we assessed whether it aligned with our project’s objectives and whether we could access or extract it sufficiently:
    • The ArCo / CIS knowledge graph is a rich semantic resource that models Italian cultural heritage with ontology modules and supports SPARQL queries. However, automatic extraction and integration were too complex for our time frame, especially given potential issues of coverage, query complexity, and alignment with our funding/visitor datasets.
    • The MiC “income/budget” datasets were initially considered as a complementary source for analysis of financial trends in the cultural sector. However, they were excluded because the available data are too aggregated: they do not distinguish between different cultural domains (e.g., heritage, libraries, performing arts) or provide consistent regional and yearly breakdowns. As a result, it was not possible to align them with the OpenCoesione data, which is organized by project, region, and year.
    • The ISTAT “Aspects of Daily Life” survey is broad and includes items about cultural consumption, but it is in the form of microdata (household-level survey), which requires careful sampling treatment, potential licensing restrictions, and may not align regionally or temporally with our main funding data (open coesione).
    Instead, we concentrated on OpenCoesione project/funding data and visitor/participation datasets from MiC, which better matched our regional-year analysis needs.
    • OpenCoesione – Culture & Tourism Project Data (2014–2020, 2021–2027) provides detailed information on individual public-funded projects, including total funding, funding source (public/private/foreign), start and end dates, and geographic scope. They are openly available and regularly updated.
    • Ministero della Cultura – Visitor and Participation Statistics publishes official statistics on annual visitors to cultural heritage sites, including museums, monuments, and archaeological areas, broken down by region and institution type. This dataset enabled us to measure regional participation in cultural heritage and to compare it with the level of public investment recorded by OpenCoesione.
    We began by extracting and organizing data from OpenCoesione (funding and project information) and Ministero della Cultura (MiC) datasets (visitor and participation statistics).

Data Cleaning

Once our scope and source datasets were selected, we proceeded to prepare them for analysis and visualization. This began with data ingestion and ended with clean, normalized CSVs ready for plotting. Steps included:

  • Ingesting raw data
    • Downloaded the OpenCoesione “Culture & Tourism” project datasets (for both 2014–2020 and 2021–2027 ranges).
    • Downloaded or retrieved visitor/participation datasets from MiC / Ufficio Statistica.
    • Stored these raw datasets(CSV), originally in xls/csv, in the data folder of the repository. (note: add the link to the repo here)
  • Deduplication & filtering
    • Removed duplicate rows (based on unique project IDs) and NaN values.
    • Filtered to only include projects with cultural heritage or tourism relevance (excluding unrelated sectors).
    • Filtered the data to years between 2014 and 2024, inclusive, to match the analysis window.
    • Date conversion & formatting
    • Transformed date columns ( ;`DATA_INIZIO_PREV_STUDIO_FATT`, `OC_DATA_INIZIO_PROGETTO`, `OC_DATA_FINE_PROGETTO_EFFETTIVA`) into datetime objects in Python, standardizing to Day-Month-Year format.
    • Extracted the year component of dates to facilitate yearly aggregations.
  • Normalization of categorical variables
    • Unified regional names (e.g. “Sicilia” vs “Sicily” or inconsistent abbreviations).
    • Harmonized project classification codes (if multiple naming schemes) into a master classification for “cultural heritage/tourism”.
    • Merging and aggregating tables
    • Joined the project data (funding, project count) with regional visitor/participation data by region + year.
  • Output to CSV for visualization
    • Exported cleaned and merged tables as CSV files in the data folder. (e.g. `open_coesione.csv`, `mic_visitors.csv`).
    At the end of this phase, we had two sets of tidy, analysis-ready CSVs with consistent region/year keys and aligned variables (funding, project counts, visitor participation), enabling the next step of creating visualizations and performing the comparisons.

Graphing Data

To answer the research questions, visualizations were produced to investigate the funding/visitor trends and their connections across Italian regions.

  • Funding allocated to cultural heritage projects in each region (2014–2024)
    • By analyzing the first general graphs, the funding source, time scope, and top-funded regions were sampled to gain a clearer view of the connection between the datasets. To achieve this, we began by mapping the numbers in the dataset to gain a clear understanding of the temporal/regional inputs that stand out. Namely, the first barchart on the notebook (fig.1.1) directed us to a narrower time scope(Top 5 years), and the regional geographical map (fig.2.1), showing the distribution of funding throughout the dataset, led us to the selected regions (Top 5 regions). To make sure the selected regions were truly the top-funded ones in recent years, we plotted a bargraph with a confined time scope to show consistency in the results (fig.3). To depict the actual trend in the allocation of funds, we used heatmaps to show the amount each region received for its cultural heritage projects each year (fig.4.1).
  • Visitor fluctuations within the years under study
    • Moving on to the number of visitors, we used the second dataset (mic_visitors), and plotted a bubblemap which showed each region's share of yearly visits (fig.6).

Conclusions

  • Top 5 Funded Years (Overall)
    • CAMPANIA, PUGLIA, SICILIA, TOSCANA, CALABRIA
    • These years record the highest total public funding for cultural heritage projects according to OpenCoesione, which tracks public investments at project level. The sharp decline in 2020–2021 corresponds to the COVID-19 pandemic, when cultural institutions were closed or operating under restrictions, leading to a temporary suspension of new projects and disbursements..
  • Top 5 Funded Regions(Overall)
    • CAMPANIA, PUGLIA, SICILIA, TOSCANA, CALABRIA
    • These regions received the largest cumulative funding over the observed period (2014–2024). Their prominence reflects ongoing investment programs targeting the southern regions, often supported by European structural funds aimed at cultural and territorial cohesion.
  • Top 5 Funded Regions in each Top 5 year:
    • CAMPANIA(2016) - CAMPANIA(2017) - PUGLIA(2018) - MOLISE(2019) - CAMPANIA(2022)
    • These annual peaks correspond to specific funding cycles or project completions visible in the OpenCoesione dataset, which aggregates values based on project start and completion dates.
  • Top 5 Visited Regions in each Top 5 year:
    • LAZIO(2016) - CAMPANIA(2017) - TOSCANA(2018) - PIEMONTE(2019) - LOMBARDIA(2022)
    • Data from MiC_Visitors (Ministero della Cultura) show that visitor flows are concentrated in regions with well-established cultural tourism circuits and major cities, particularly Lazio (Rome) and Toscana (Florence).
